## Court Name: Supreme Court of India

## Petitioner Name(s): B.N. John and Anr.

## Respondent Name(s): State of U.P. & Anr.

## Case Background: The case revolves around the FIR filed by District Probation Officer, Varanasi, against the appellant, B.N. John, under Section 353 of the IPC for allegedly using criminal force to prevent public servants from discharging their official duties during a raid on his hostel premises. However, it is argued that the FIR does not disclose the ingredients of the offence under Section 353 of the IPC and was filed without the order of a Judicial Magistrate.

## Petitioner Arguments: The petitioner argues that the FIR does not mention any specific acts apart from alleging "creating disturbance", which cannot be construed as "assault" or "criminal force" within the scope of Section 353 of the IPC. It is also argued that the absence of mentioning these vital facts in the FIR would vitiate the cognizance taken by the CJM.

## Respondent Argument: The respondent argues that the police can continue with the investigation of a non-cognizable offence if new facts emerge during the investigation of a cognizable offence, and that the initial process being vitiated does not affect the subsequent process.

## Issues discussed in Court: The court discusses the issues of whether the FIR discloses the ingredients of the offence under Section 353 of the IPC, whether the absence of mentioning these vital facts in the FIR would vitiate the cognizance taken by the CJM, and whether the police can continue with the investigation of a non-cognizable offence.

## Final Decision: The court allows the appeal and quashes Case No. 9790 of 2015 arising out of Case Crime No. 290 of 2015 under Sections 353 and 186 of the IPC, pending before the Court of the CJM, Varanasi.

## Court's Reasoning: The court holds that taking cognizance by the CJM, Varanasi, of the offences under Section 353 of the IPC and 186 of the IPC was not done by following the due process contemplated under the provisions of law, and accordingly, the same being contrary to law, all the orders passed pursuant thereto cannot be sustained.
