## Court Name: Supreme Court of India
## Petitioner Name(s): Ayush Yardi and similarly placed persons
## Respondent Name(s): State of Madhya Pradesh
## Case Background: The petitioners, Ayush Yardi and similarly placed persons, challenged the rules of the Madhya Pradesh Judicial Service (Recruitment and Conditions of Service) Rules, 1994, which excluded visually impaired candidates from applying for judicial service posts. The petitioners argued that the rules violated their rights under the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016.

## Petitioner Arguments:
The petitioners contended that the rules were discriminatory and denied them equal opportunities in the selection process. They also argued that the rules did not provide reasonable accommodations for visually impaired candidates, which was a requirement under the RPwD Act, 2016.

## Respondent Argument:
The State of Madhya Pradesh argued that the rules were necessary to ensure the integrity of the judicial service and that visually impaired candidates were not suitable for the posts due to their disability.

## Issues discussed in Court:
* Whether the rules violated the rights of persons with disabilities under the RPwD Act, 2016.
* Whether visually impaired candidates should be excluded from applying for judicial service posts.
* The importance of reasonable accommodations for visually impaired candidates.

## Final Decision:
The Supreme Court struck down Rule 6A of the Madhya Pradesh Judicial Service (Recruitment and Conditions of Service) Rules, 1994, as it excluded visually impaired candidates. It also struck down Rule 7 of the same rules to the extent that it prescribed additional requirements for visually impaired candidates.

## Court's Reasoning:
The court held that visually impaired candidates should not be discriminated against and that reasonable accommodations should be provided to ensure their equal participation in the selection process. The court also emphasized the importance of affirmative action on behalf of the State to provide an inclusive framework for persons with disabilities.
